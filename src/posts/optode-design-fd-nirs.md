One of the engineering problems I have been working on is how to improve optical coupling for frequency-domain near-infrared spectroscopy in participants with Afro-textured hair. This challenge is more demanding than it first appears, because many existing interface solutions were developed for continuous-wave systems and do not meet the design constraints required by frequency-domain measurements.

Continuous-wave near-infrared spectroscopy primarily depends on maintaining local optode–scalp contact. In contrast, frequency-domain near-infrared spectroscopy requires not only stable contact but also stable multi-distance geometry, accurate spacing across multiple source–detector separations, and phase- and amplitude-consistent coupling over time. The design logic therefore has to move beyond simple contact preservation and toward geometric stability across the full measurement module.  [oai_citation:0‡SPIE_Photonics_West_draft_v0.pdf] 

My current approach is a module-centric design built around one detector and four sources. The source–detector arrangement includes one short pair and additional separations spanning approximately 0.5 to 3.5 centimeters, so the module can support the spacing requirements of frequency-domain measurements while remaining mechanically compact. The design includes a main clip and a top clip, with side prongs intended to engage with braids and improve traction.  [oai_citation:1‡SPIE_Photonics_West_draft_v0.pdf] 

A major idea in this design is to treat the hairstyle not simply as an obstacle, but as part of the attachment strategy. In braided configurations, the clip can anchor into the hair structure itself, using prongs with tooth-like features to improve grip and reduce motion. This is different from many earlier designs that focus mainly on pressing the optode against the scalp. Here, the goal is to stabilize the entire source–detector module while preserving the geometry required by frequency-domain near-infrared spectroscopy.  [oai_citation:2‡SPIE_Photonics_West_draft_v0.pdf] 

The early evaluation was carried out in five healthy adults with Afro-textured hair, including both aligned braids and unbraided afros with varying density. Hair density was characterized using standardized scalp imaging with a DermLite Handyscope. To assess signal quality, I estimated signal-to-noise ratio from the cardiac component of the oxyhemoglobin signal using time–frequency ridge analysis, and I also examined oxygen saturation estimates derived at the cardiac frequency.  [oai_citation:3‡SPIE_Photonics_West_draft_v0.pdf] 

One practical result stood out clearly: the cardiac signal was preserved only when coupling was good. In low-density braided hair, the cardiac peak was isolated clearly above background fluctuations. In high-density unbraided hair, the same peak blended into surrounding spectral activity, indicating poor coupling. This made the cardiac component a useful physiological marker of whether the interface was working as intended.  [oai_citation:4‡SPIE_Photonics_West_draft_v0.pdf] 

At this stage, the project is still an engineering iteration rather than a finished solution. The sample is small, and the design will need more testing across a wider range of hairstyles and densities. But the early pattern is encouraging: for frequency-domain near-infrared spectroscopy, interface design needs to be driven by measurement geometry and coupling stability together, and braided attachment may offer a practical route toward more inclusive and reliable optical sensing.
